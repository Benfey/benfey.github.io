title: "The End of Reality"
date: 2026-03-12

---

Somewhere right now, millions of people are watching something that never happened, shared by someone who doesn't exist, and feeling something real about all of it.

Two in three people on Earth use social media[^1]. The feed is the lens through which most of humanity now sees the world.

And increasingly, what comes through that lens never happened at all.

34 million AI-generated images are produced every day[^2], and we can't tell them apart from the real thing.

And that's just images. AI-generated video is on the same trajectory. On TikTok alone, 354 automated AI accounts generated 4.5 billion views in a single month. TikTok labeled 1.38% of it[^3].

The global information supply is being quietly contaminated, and the filters we thought we had don't work.

A photograph used to be evidence. That assumption is now broken, and everything downstream of it is breaking too. Journalism, legal evidence, public trust, personal reputation. All of it relied on a basic contract: that an image reflects something that happened. That contract is void.

Political deepfakes are already circulating during elections. Fabricated atrocity footage provokes real-world violence. AI-generated scam ads feature people who never consented. And the tools are getting cheaper, faster, and more accessible by the month.

The counterargument is always detection. AI watermarking, forensic tools, platform labeling. But the TikTok numbers tell the real story: 4.5 billion views, 1.38% labeled. Detection is not keeping pace, and there's no reason to believe it will. The asymmetry is structural. Generating a fake image takes seconds. Verifying one takes infrastructure, policy, and cooperation that doesn't exist yet.

Meanwhile, 5.24 billion people scroll their feeds every day[^1]. The average person spends two and a half hours on social media daily, consuming content they have no reliable way to verify.

What happens to a society that can't agree on what's real? We're about to find out. This isn't a problem of media literacy or better labeling. It's a structural collapse of the relationship between seeing and believing. That relationship held for the entire history of photography and video. It's ending now, in real time, and we don't have a replacement.

Where do we go from here?
